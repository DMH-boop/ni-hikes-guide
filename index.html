06 — Go safely

Before every walk

Based on the NZ Mountain Safety Council’s Land Safety Code and DOC guidance. This website is a planning aid — it does not replace official track information, current forecasts, or your own preparation and judgement.

⛅ 1 · Choose the right trip

Match the track to the least experienced person in your group. Winter (roughly May–October) turns Tongariro, Tama Lakes, Kime and Powell into alpine trips needing ice axe, crampons and the skills to use them.

🌧 2 · Understand the weather

Check the MetService mountain/national-park forecast the day before and the morning of your walk. NZ alpine weather changes in minutes; if a severe watch or warning is issued, change your plan.

🎒 3 · Pack warm clothes + extra food

Waterproof shell, warm insulating layers (no cotton), hat and gloves, headtorch, first aid, survival bag, and a day’s spare food — on every track graded moderate or harder in this guide.

📍 4 · Share your plans + take ways to get help

Leave trip intentions with a trusted contact (use Plan My Walk or AdventureSmart). Carry a charged phone and a distress beacon (PLB) where coverage is patchy — most of these tracks have dead zones.

🥾 5 · Take care of yourself and your group

Eat, drink, and check each other. Set a turnaround time and honour it — summits and tarns will still be there next weekend. Rivers: if you can’t see the bottom or it’s above your knees, don’t cross; wait or turn back.

🔥 Alerts, fires & closures

  • Check DOC alerts and Plan My Walk the week you travel.
  • Check fire season status before any camp stove use outdoors.
  • Respect rāhui and cultural restrictions — currently relevant at Tongariro.
Emergency: dial 111 and ask for Police — they coordinate land search and rescue in New Zealand. No signal? Activate your PLB / satellite messenger SOS. Text 111 works only if pre-registered. For volcanic activity levels see GeoNet.
